What does CRC stand for?

CRC stands for Certified Risk Adjustment Coder (AAPC). It‚Äôs a credential focused on risk adjustment coding concepts such as HCCs, documentation requirements, and the impact of diagnosis coding accuracy on risk scores.

In plain terms: the CRC certification validates that you understand how to code for risk adjustment and how documentation and ICD-10-CM selection influence risk scoring.

Who should take the CRC exam?

The CRC exam is a great fit if you‚Äôre:

  • A medical coder moving into (or already working in) risk adjustment
  • Supporting Medicare Advantage, value-based care, auditing, or coding compliance
  • A coder who wants to strengthen HCC coding and documentation knowledge
  • Preparing for a role that involves chart review, suspecting, recapture, or provider education

What topics are on the CRC exam?

While exam blueprints can evolve, CRC prep typically includes these core areas:

  • Risk adjustment fundamentals and why it matters
  • HCC basics: categories, hierarchy concepts, and chronic condition capture
  • ICD-10-CM coding guidelines (especially for chronic conditions)
  • Documentation standards, MEAT, and common provider documentation gaps
  • Compliance, audits, and best practices for accurate reporting
  • Case-based scenarios and coding application

Many coders find CRC challenging because it tests both coding knowledge and risk adjustment application. It‚Äôs less about memorization and more about understanding what documentation supports a diagnosis and how to apply ICD-10-CM accurately.

How to prepare for the CRC exam

Step 1: Refresh your risk adjustment foundation

  • Know what risk adjustment is and why accurate coding matters
  • Understand how chronic conditions are documented and validated

Step 2: Strengthen your HCC and ICD-10-CM accuracy

  • Focus on chronic condition specificity and guideline-driven coding
  • Practice identifying documentation gaps and what to query

Step 3: Practice with CRC-style questions

  • Do timed sets (to build pacing)
  • Review rationales (why the answer is correct)
  • Track weak topics and repeat practice until consistent
